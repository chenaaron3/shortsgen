You are predicting which of two short-form video scripts would get more views if shown to the same audience with similar packaging, timing, and distribution.

Your job is NOT to judge which script is wiser, deeper, more practical, more factual, more impressive, or better written.
Your job is to predict which script is more likely to make viewers keep watching and feel enough payoff to share, save, or comment.

Script A and Script B are assigned randomly. Do not favor either position.

Read both scripts fully before deciding.

Evaluate each script independently first, then compare.

Use these criteria:

1. Stop-scroll pull

- Do the first 1–2 lines create a strong reason to keep watching?
- This can come from curiosity, recognition, tension, surprise, relevance, or emotional precision.

2. Felt recognition

- Does the script make the viewer feel seen, understood, or called out in a compelling way?
- A script can win because it feels emotionally exact, not just because it is useful.

3. Center of gravity

- Does the script feel like it is driving toward one dominant promise, feeling, or reframe?
- This does NOT require only one tactic or one example.
- A broader script can still be strong if its parts clearly ladder into one emotional or conceptual payoff.
- Penalize only when the script feels scattered, stacked, or like advice soup.

4. Payoff satisfaction

- Does the script deliver a payoff that feels earned?
- The payoff can be:
  - a practical technique
  - a memorable reframe
  - emotional relief
  - a sharper self-understanding
  - a quotable worldview shift
- Do not favor technique payoffs over emotional or identity payoffs by default.

5. Share impulse

- Would someone send this to a friend, save it, or comment on it?
- This can happen because:
  - “this is useful”
  - “this is me”
  - “this is you”
  - “I needed to hear this”
  - “this line is so true”

Important anti-bias rules:

- Do NOT systematically favor practical scripts over reflective scripts.
- Do NOT systematically favor emotional scripts over practical scripts.
- Do NOT assume the better script is the one with the most concrete tactic.
- Do NOT assume the better script is the one with the most profound tone.
- Do NOT over-reward neuroscience, statistics, named frameworks, or productivity language.
- Do NOT over-reward “identity” or “authenticity” language just because it sounds deep.
- Judge actual viewer pull and payoff, not surface sophistication.

Common loser patterns:

- generic “you’ve been lied to” framing without a strong turn
- stacked frameworks or too many pivots
- fake precision or bolted-on neuroscience
- empty intensity
- broad advice with no memorable line
- a hook that promises more than the body delivers

When uncertain, prefer the script with the stronger combination of:

- stop-scroll pull
- emotional or practical relevance
- memorable center of gravity
- payoff that feels most sendable or saveable in real life

Output exactly:
Winner: A or B
Reason: 1–2 sentences explaining the main viewer-behavior reason.
